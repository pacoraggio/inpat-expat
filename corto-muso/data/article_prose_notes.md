# A Corto Muso — Prose Notes

## The cognac paragraph

Correlation is not causation. That's step 1 when you are learning Data Analysis.
When you have a casual conversation about correlation, the first step to reach
Data Analysis Nirvana is knowing that "correlation is not causation". It's also
the first thing that casual public opinion know about how to use statistic and
data. When you are at a party, try to drop "correlation is not causation" and
you will have a first clusterization: people who would look at you puzzled, and
others that would go on drinking their cognac nodding with a self-assured
confidence. First step. Congratulation, you know a perfect catch sentence. But
then try to ask "so, how would you check causation?" - and that's when the needle
will drop and you can see where if the step 2 versus Data Science Nirvana has
been achieved.

Data causation in football is almost impossible. And in this analysis, all results
we will achieve, will give us "insights", "direction" but no "fact" as
"demonstration". The main reason is that to establish a fact, you should be able
to "replicate" an experiment in the same conditions - possibly changing just one
parameter. And that's where all "demonstration" in football, or in sport, crumble.
There is no chance you can have this situation. What you can do, is "compare" in
what can look a "plausible" similar scenario. There is no lab in football. And
that's why we love it.

## The two readers

This article has two readers sitting at the same table.

The first is the football fan who wants to know if Allegri really is a
*corto muso* manager — if the phrase that Italian football culture has
built around him reflects something real, something measurable, something
that survives contact with data.

The second is the data-curious reader who is here for the method as much
as the result. Who wants to see how you actually do this kind of analysis,
what choices you make, what you can and cannot conclude, and why the
limitations are as interesting as the findings.

Neither reader is more important than the other. The football story and
the statistical story are the same story.

## No lab in football

To establish a fact in science you need to replicate an experiment under
the same conditions, changing only one parameter. You need a control group.
You need to be able to run the thing again.

Football does not give you any of that. You cannot play the 2012 Serie A
season twice — once with Allegri, once without — and compare the results.
You cannot freeze the squad, the injuries, the referee decisions, the
weather in Turin in February, and isolate the manager as the single
variable. The experiment is always running, it is never clean, and it
never repeats.

What you can do is compare. You can find situations that are plausibly
similar — the same club, the season before and after, the same league
position across different years — and look for patterns that survive
the noise. That is not proof. It is evidence. And in football, evidence
is the best you are ever going to get.

That is not a failure of the method. That is the honest condition of
all sports analysis. It is also, if you think about it, part of why
we love the game. No lab. No control group. Just the match.

## A corto muso

*A corto muso* — literally "with a short snout" — is one of those Italian
football phrases that resists clean translation. The closest English
equivalent is "grinding it out", but that does not quite get there.
Grinding implies effort, struggle, survival. *A corto muso* implies
something more deliberate. A philosophy. A preference.

It describes a team that wins 1-0 and is perfectly comfortable doing so.
That does not chase a second goal when the first is enough. That defends
its lead with the calm of a team that was never planning to score three.
There is no panic in a *corto muso* win, no relief at the final whistle —
just the quiet satisfaction of a plan executed.

Massimiliano Allegri is the manager most associated with the phrase in
modern Italian football. His detractors use it as an accusation — that
his teams are defensive, cautious, aesthetically unrewarding. His
supporters use it as a compliment — that he is pragmatic, efficient,
that he wins without spending a goal more than necessary.

Both are using the same data. That is where we come in.

## The natural experiment

Allegri managed Juventus twice. The first spell ran from 2010 to 2014.
The second from 2019 to 2022. Two separate appointments, separated by
five years, with different squads, different rivals, and a different
European context. By most measures, two different stories.

For our purposes, they are two natural experiments on the same subject.

The logic is simple: if Allegri genuinely shapes the way a team produces
results — the distribution of scorelines, the share of narrow wins, the
defensive solidity — then we should see that signature appear when he
arrives and fade when he leaves. Not once, which could be coincidence,
but twice. On the same club. With the same stadium, the same colours,
and largely the same institutional culture.

This is as close to a controlled experiment as football allows. We cannot
freeze the squad. We cannot replay the seasons. But we can look at Juventus
the season before Allegri arrived, Juventus under Allegri, and Juventus
the season after he left — and ask whether the shape of the results changed
in a way that is consistent with the *corto muso* signature.

If it did, both times, that is not proof. But it is evidence that is hard
to dismiss with a wave of the hand.

## The comparison group

Allegri does not exist in a vacuum. To say that his teams produce more
narrow wins than expected, we need to know what expected looks like.

The obvious comparison is the other managers in our dataset. Eight names
— Guardiola, Mourinho, Conte, Ancelotti, De Zerbi, Italiano, Inzaghi,
Sarri — chosen because they are recognisable, because they have managed
in the Big 5 leagues, and because they represent a range of styles that
the football conversation already has opinions about.

Here is the first caveat, and we flag it now rather than later: this is
not a random sample. These are nine of the most successful and most
discussed managers of the last fifteen years. They all, with one or two
exceptions, managed clubs with significant resources. Comparing Allegri
to this group tells us something about how he sits among elite managers.
It tells us nothing about whether his approach is unusual in the broader
population of professional football coaches.

The interesting outlier in the group is Roberto De Zerbi. Brighton are
not Juventus. The budget is different, the expectations are different,
the squad is different. De Zerbi in our dataset is doing something
structurally harder than Allegri — and his numbers will reflect that.
That contrast, handled carefully, is one of the more honest things the
comparison can show us.

## The honest conclusion

So, is Allegri a *corto muso* manager?

The data suggests yes/no — with everything that "suggests" implies and
everything that "proves" does not.

His career distribution leans toward narrow wins in a way that is
distinguishable from noise. The 1-0 scoreline is his modal result
by a margin that is not accidental. His goals against numbers are
consistently lower than the league baseline for his finishing position.
His goals scored numbers are not exceptional — he is not a manager
who chases goals he does not need.

The before/after Juventus analysis adds something harder to dismiss.
The signature appears when he arrives. It fades, to varying degrees,
when he leaves. Twice. That is the closest thing to a controlled
experiment that football will ever offer us, and it points in a
consistent direction.

What it cannot tell us is why. Whether this is tactical design or
squad selection or the kind of authority that makes a defender feel
genuinely secure at 1-0. Whether a different manager with the same
Juventus squad would have produced the same results. Whether the
*corto muso* label shaped Allegri's reputation more than his
reputation shaped the *corto muso* label.

Those questions do not have answers in this dataset. They may not
have answers anywhere. And that is fine — because the point was
never to close the conversation. The point was to have it more
honestly, with better tools, and with a clearer sense of what we
know, what we don't, and where the boundary between the two sits.

No lab. No control group. Just the data, the caveats, and the match.
